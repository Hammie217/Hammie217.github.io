Electric Fields and Potential

If we place a charged particle into a region where it experiences an electrostatic interaction (i.e. other charges are present), two interesting effects occur.

1) The particle gains (or loses) potential energy when it is moved to this position. We can see this if we let the particle be mobile, it will experience a force (see below) and therefore move. What we are seeing is the potential energy being converted to kinetic energy. The energy of the particle depends on the magnitude of its charge and a property of the space in which it is sitting. This property is called "potential", and exists even if the particle is not present. It is caused by the other charges that are present. Potential is a scalar quantity (i.e. it has no direction) and can be thought of as analogous to height in a gravitational system.

2) The particle experiences an electrostatic force. This force depends on the particle's charge and a quantity called the "electric field". This electric field has both a magnitude and a direction and is therefore a vector quantity that varies with position. In mathematical jargon a scalar quantity that varies with position -like potential- is often called a "scalar field" and a vector quantity that varies with position is a "vector field". Don't confuse this use of the word field with the use we will use, where field means the electric field.
