EEE317 Principles of Communications

Video for Mobile Phones
- an overview of mobile TV for cellular handsets and the schemes that will be used to implement it.

Mobile video will be one of the next major selling points for mobile phones. Although third generation systems have already promised the possibility of downloadable videos, in reality, use of the cellular telecommunications infrastructure to provide large amounts of data to a single user will be too expensive for most people to use it freely. Instead the possibility of using broadcast techniques is now coming to the fore.

It is anticipated that mobile broadcast TV will take off in a major way. Early indications from countries such as Korea where major trials are under way have been very promising. Korea also offered short clips of the Olympic Games over the 3G network, and these proved to be popular. Indications are that once accepted, mobile video on mobile phones will become a "must have" feature for many. For operators it represents a significant opportunity to increase revenues, although they will be careful not to let it impact the revenues from their core 3G mobile phone networks, and this may impose some restrictions on the facilities available.

 
Advantages of broadcast

The concept of broadcasting to many users simultaneously offers many advantages over sending individual transmissions to different users, typically called a one-to-one communications system. Costs of downloading videos will need to be paid by the user, and therefore may be large. If a broadcast style model is used, which may be thought of as a one-to-many communications link, then the delivery costs are much lower. The disadvantage is that the level of choice is reduced to what is being broadcast. Nevertheless it is this business model that looks most attractive and the one that is most likely to succeed for video and general mobile content. The high data rates of 3G can then be reserved for content such as data downloads, data communications, video conferencing and the like.

 
Main systems

As might be expected, more than one system has been developed. Around the world four standards are appearing, two of which it appears will have widespread international support and will have open standards.

  • T-DMB (Terrestrial Digital Multimedia Broadcasting)
  • DVB-H (Digital Video Broadcast - Handheld)
  • ISDB-T (Integrated Services Digital Broadcasting - Terrestrial)
  • Mediaflow

Of these T-DMB is based on the DAB (Digital Audio Broadcasting) system which is currently gaining significant support in the UK and other countries around the world. DVB-H is based upon the DVB-T terrestrial television broadcasting system used in the UK and many other countries. ISDB-T is a standard that is only being used in Japan, and finally Mediaflow is a scheme that is being developed by Qualcomm. Mediaflow is a registered trade name of Qualcomm

 
Business models

The exact way in which mobile video will be implemented as far as revenue is concerned will depend on the operators. There may be subscription services and there may also be services that are supported by advertising. The huge advantage to placing video onto mobile phones is that people already carry their phones in their pockets. If it is possible to extend their functionality to include video, this would mean an additional device is not required. Additionally the billing infrastructure is already in place, so this too can be extended without the need to start again.

It is possible that mobile video transmissions could also be used for other services. These could include traffic and weather reports broadcast in the background and brought up as required on the mobile. Additionally, broadcast technology could be used for software upgrades, allowing mobiles to be upgraded, and new features added, online.

Yet Mobile video in this format is not the only way in which video can be displayed on a mobile phone. For example, the Orange MobiTV system sends ITV and CNN news over the 3G network and this can be viewed by users with a Nokia 6680 handset. Spectrum considerations

One of the big issues surrounding mobile video transmissions is that of radio spectrum. As these transmissions will not use the bands already allocated for cellular communications, further bandwidth will be needed. This is likely to delay the introduction of mobile video services in some countries.

In an ideal world it would be advantageous to allocate the same bands for mobile video broadcasting worldwide. In reality this is unlikely to happen totally, although there will undoubtedly be a large degree of commonality, although it is likely that not all countries will be able to adopt the same bands. In addition to this there are no bands set aside for DVB-H broadcasting, although for DMB, the technology is sufficiently similar to utilise the bands allocated for broadcasting using DAB.

In the long term it is anticipated that as the UHF analogue transmissions are closed this will release vast amounts of valuable spectrum and some of this could be allocated to mobile video broadcasts. In some countries this could result in spectrum auctions, although the UK regulatory authority, Ofcom, will want to ensure that a repeat of the 3G auctions in 2000 is not repeated as these are recognised to have damaged the cellular industry.

System comparisons

It is worth comparing the various systems for downloading video which are being trialled.

  System Strengths Weaknesses  
 

Streaming video over cellular network
  • No significant added complexity for handset
  • Return path provided for interactivity
  • System not designed to provide broadcast content
  • Provides significant extra load on network structure
  • Significant increase in network signalling traffic
  • Bandwidth is limited, even for 3G
  
  DMB
(DAB)
  • DAB is established, with an open standard
  • Infrastructure exists in several countries
  • Spectrum already available in some countries
  • DAB originally designed for a mobile environment
  • Only decodes part of the transmission at a time, making it more efficient in terms of memory and power consumption.
  • Only requires a small antenna
  • In some countries (UK) licence restrictions restrict 20% of available capacity for data services
  • Frequency allocations vary according to country and this may restrict roaming
  • Alternative Satellite based - S-DMB services are expensive to build and run
  • No return path for interactive operation
  • Will increase size, weight and power consumptions of phones
  
 

DVB-H
  • Established standard based on terrestrial DVB-T broadcast standard
  • Spectral efficiency greater than DMB
  • Uses advanced codecs
  • Has considerable industry support e.g. Nokia
  • Content can be broadcast in its original format
  • Only requires a small antenna
  • Entire transmission must be decoded before a particular channel can be extracted
  • Higher power consumption than DMB
  • Requires more transmitter sites than the number needed for DVB-T (but fewer than the number needed for cellular communications)
  • Spectrum not allocated or available yet
  • No return path for interactive operation
  • High chip costs
  • Will increase size, weight and power consumptions of phones
  

 
Outlook

It remains to be seen whether DMB or DVB-H will become the major standard adopted for mobile video, or whether both schemes may be used in different countries around the world. DMB trials are well advanced, particularly in Korea where it appears DMB will be adopted. DVB-H, however, has the support of some major industry players, has shown success in some of the trials undertaken, and according to the general industry vibes could become the dominant standard.

Many of the chipset developers are leaving the way open for the use of multiple standard chips capable of receiving both DVB-H and DMB, and even switching between the various bands which may be in use around the globe. Despite the tough challenge, the developers maintain that they are able to provide low power chipsets, suitable for incorporation into the latest mobile phones.

It remains to be seen exactly how this new area of the mobile phone industry will develop, but it is certain that mobile TV will play a big part.
