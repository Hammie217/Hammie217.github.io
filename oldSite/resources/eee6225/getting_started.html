EEE6225 Systems Design

Getting Started
1. Introduction
Your task is to work in a small team to carry out a piece of digital design work.  The specific task is design and implement the Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) on an FPGA board to meet some constraint (low area or high throughput).

At the start of this exercise you are confronted with a large amount of information and the realisation that this is an opportunity to develop your skills in a number of areas and it may seem quite bewildering.  Your immediate thoughts are likely to be:

What is the AES?
How do I design digital circuits?
What is VHDL?
Am I really expected to do this?
Who is in my group?
What are we supposed to be doing?
How do we get started?

D O N T    P A N I C  !   This is normal, you are not expected to know everything on the first day!
2. Planning
It is essential to plan what you as a group are going to do and regularly review the plan.  Planning allows you to be much more effective as a team.  You need to start by breaking down the "big scary" task into simpler tasks each with objectives, dependencies and time-scales.  It is good practice to assign responsibility for a task to a member of the group, with an agreed deadline for its completion.   Larger tasks should be broken down into stages (each being its own task) with a review process put in place to guide overall delivery of a successful outcome for the larger task.

NOTE: Planning itself is your first TASK and an ongoing one requiring regular review!
3. The Design Process
Digital design is a process and follows the same method some may already be familiar with for software.  There are a number of distinct stages each followed by a review and if necessary rework (which may include revisiting an earlier step).
  • Requirements capture: what the resulting module should do from its users perspective (in plain language).
  • Specification: typically quantified detail (in engineering language) of what the module must do and what is "nice to have".
  • Outline design: using not too many words and ideally a diagram or picture and overview of your solution.
  • Detail design: this is where attention to detail is necessary and all the "what if..." questions need answering.  In doing so remember to document them as they may form part of the testing.
  • Testing: this is where you prove that your design does what is required by the specification.  Early detection of problems will reduce the cost (your time!) to fix them. 
  • Delivery: typically a design is always part of a larger system and must be handed over to someone else to integrate it into the bigger project.  This will certainly include documentation of what it does and how to check it works.
  • Critical review: this is an essential part of engineering and how we as engineers can improve our engineering decision making process.  Ask yourself questions like: How well was the task done?  What could I have done better?  What have I learned in the process?  What should I make a note of to do differently next time?

NOTE: Design is hierarchical, there are many different levels all of which have interfaces and interact with each other.  Changes at any one level can drastically effect other parts of the system.
4. Teamwork
Teamwork is all about communication!  As a group you need to coordinate your efforts and consider how and what you are doing will affect the other members of your group.  You need to consider what you need from other group members AND what they need from you!

Early formulation of a clear plan and ensuring that changes to the plan are discussed and communicated to all group members makes the overall task much simpler.  Continually reviewing your plan does not mean continually changing it, once a plan is agreed stick to it until you have fully considered the need for changing it.

If you think you need to drastically change your plan during semester 2 discuss this with your demonstrators or academic staff first.
